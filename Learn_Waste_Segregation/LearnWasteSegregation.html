Waste Wise: Learn Waste Segregation

Why Segregate Waste?

Waste segregation is the process of dividing waste into different categories (e.g., wet, dry, recyclable, hazardous) at the source. This seemingly simple act has a profound impact on our environment, public health, and economy.

  • Environmental Protection: Reduces landfill burden and prevents soil/water contamination.
  • Resource Recovery: Facilitates recycling and composting, conserving natural resources.
  • Public Health: Minimizes disease transmission and improves sanitation.
  • Economic Benefits: Creates jobs in the recycling industry and reduces waste management costs.

For residents of Visakhapatnam, proper waste segregation plays a crucial role in supporting the city's cleanliness initiatives and smart city goals. By separating your waste at home, you directly contribute to a healthier and more sustainable environment for our community.

Understand Waste Categories

Wet Waste / Organic Waste

Biodegradable waste, typically from kitchens and gardens. It can be composted to create nutrient-rich soil.

Examples:

  • Food scraps (fruits, vegetables, cooked food)
  • Garden waste (leaves, grass clippings, small branches)
  • Flower waste
  • Meat and fish bones (in some composting systems)

Goes into: Green Bin

Dry Waste / Recyclables

Non-biodegradable waste that can often be recycled. Proper segregation is crucial for efficient recycling.

Examples:

  • Paper (newspapers, magazines, cardboard)
  • Plastics (bottles, containers - look for recycling symbols)
  • Metal (cans, foils)
  • Glass (bottles, jars)
  • Textiles (old clothes, rags)

Goes into: Blue Bin

Hazardous Waste

Waste that poses a substantial or potential threat to public health or the environment. Requires special disposal.

Examples:

  • Batteries (AA, AAA, button cells, vehicle batteries)
  • Light bulbs (CFLs, fluorescent tubes)
  • E-waste (old electronics, wires, chargers)
  • Paints, solvents, chemicals
  • Medicines (expired drugs)
  • Pesticides

Goes into: Red/Yellow Bin (Special Collection)

Sanitary Waste

Waste that is not directly hazardous but requires careful disposal for hygiene reasons.

Examples:

  • Sanitary pads, diapers
  • Medical dressings
  • Contaminated cotton

Goes into: Black Bin (or separate collection)
